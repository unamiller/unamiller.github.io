A high-resolution process study of High Salinity Shelf Water formation in the Terra Nova Bay Polynya, Ross Sea, Antarctica

Una Miller*, Christopher J. Zappa*, Seung Tae Yoon**, Craig Stevens***, Won Sang Lee**, Arnold Gordon*

*Lamont-Doherty Earth Observatory | Columbia University
**Korean Polar Research Institute  ***National Institute of Water and Atmospheric Research

High Salinity Shelf Water is...

  • ... a primary component of Antarctic Bottom Water (AABW), a water mass that drives global meridional overturning circulation
  • ...defined as having a salinity > 34.8 with a temperature below that of freezing point at the surface (Orsi & Wiederwohl, 2009)
  • ...formed via brine rejection in the coastal polynyas of Antarctica

Despite its relatively small size, the Terra Nova Bay polynya in the Western Ross Sea produces 33% of the total Ross sea HSSW volume (Jendersie et al., 2018) as well as the densest variety of HSSW in Antarctica.

Source: NASA Aqua MODIS visible imagery

Data collection

Motivation: Because of the difficulties associated with collecting data in Antarctica outside of austral summer months, we lack in-situ data with which we can better understand the mechanisms surrounding HSSW formation. Ross Sea HSSW is of particular interest because it has been observed freshening at a rate of 0.03 per decade from 1958 to 2008, followed by an abrupt reversal of this trend from 2008 onwards (Castagno et al., 2019). Because Ross Sea HSSW makes up ~25% of AABW (Orsi et al., 2002), changes in its properties can be global in impact.

Mooring design & deployment: From February 2017 - March 2018, a mooring was deployed on a local bathymetric high in 390 meters water depth, located 8 kilometers offshore of automatic weather station (AWS) Manuela on the Nansen Ice Shelf. Both the AWS and mooring were in the direct path of katabatic winds channeled down the Reeves glacier.

The mooring was designed to capture the properties and movement of HSSW from the surface to depth at high spatial and temporal resolution. These are some of the shallowest year-round measurements of their kind to date.

 

Year overview

Over the year, there were:

  • 145 katabatic wind events, defined here as wind speeds over 25 m/s sustained for at least one hour
  • 61 HSSW production events, defined here by the presence of water with salinity > 34.8 at the shallowest CTD (53m), sustained for at least one hour

 

The lowest salinity of 32.61 is seen in the surface waters in March 2017 corresponding to the accumulation of meltwater over the austral summer. The highest salinity of 34.89 is associated with HSSW production during the austral winter. (The low salinity seen at the surface in July 2017 is due to a chunk of ice melting directly on the mooring line and should be ignored).

The coldest temperature of -2.1 C is found at depth in the austral summer. The warmest temperatures around -0.4 C are found at the surface in December 2017.

There are 4 major water masses in Terra Nova Bay, found at the mooring site in varying amounts across depth and time.

The dotted blue line indicates freezing point at the surface (53 m). The gray lines indicate isopycnals.

Seasonal overview

Summer (December - February):

Example period: February 15-28, 2017
 

  • Terra Nova Bay is ice-free (no polynya yet); Aqua/Terra MODIS satellite ice temperatures show open water (light green: T > 265K) throughout this two week period

 

  • Strong stratification in water column, with warm, fresh Antarctic Surface Water evident in surface layer in T-S diagram

  • Enhanced E-W velocities throughout the upper 150 meters of the water column occur with wind speeds nearing 40 m/s

  • Horizontal currents at depth flow opposite the direction of the wind and surface currents
  • Vertical velocities are predominantly upwards, with greater upwelling velocities corresponding to stronger wind speeds and therefore greater surface divergence. Alternative upwelling and downwelling velocities may be the result of Langmuir circulation cells oscillating over the mooring site

Autumn (March - May):

Example period: May 1-15, 2017

  • MODIS imagery shows periods of polynya opening, with relatively warm open water (light green) surrounded by cold ice (blue)
  • The polynya varies in size based on the strength and duration of katabatic wind events

  • As described in Rusciano et al. (2013), high winds in the autumn/early winter increase salinity in the surface layer and reduce stratification, but do not yet produce HSSW

  • Increases in salinity can be seen in the above depth profiles during sustained high winds, followed by quick returns to less saline surface waters as winds die down and ice production/brine rejection slows

Winter (June - August):

Example period: July 5-20, 2017

  • Polyna continues to open and close with katabatic winds

  • HSSW production begins (hatched contours below)

 

  • Continental current profiler battery failed; no further current measurements at 50-200 meters depth

Spring (September - November):

Example period: October 25-November 15

  • Polynya opening/closing and HSSW production continue well into spring

  • However, more frequent periods of low winds (< 5 m/s) limit HSSW production

    • Eg. The satellite image above shows the polynya beginning to ice over on Nov. 3 (light blue indicating thin ice). The figure below shows HSSW production ceasing several days later, as low winds do not keep the polynya open for futher ice production

  • As summer approaches, persistent seasonal stratification returns with warm, fresh water at the surface, disallowing further HSSW production

 

 

HSSW event close-up

Here, we highlight a single HSSW production event to illustrate the fine-scale analysis this study will continue to develop.

HSSW Event #27

Production began on August 27 and continued for two weeks (shown hatched, below).

 

How does HSSW production begin?

The polynya remains open for a full week before HSSW production begins, as indicated by the relatively warm surface temperatures of open water and thin ice (green and light blue, respecitvely) seen in Aqua/Terra MODIS satellite imagery. Black indicates masked clouds.

The below figure shows our temperature and salinity measurements as discrete-depth timeseries for the 10 days leading up to the HSSW event of interest. Also shown are the latent and sensible heat fluxes calculated using the COARE bulk algorithm (Fairall et al., 1996, 2003).

The temperature throughout the water column drops following the initial opening of the polynya on August 20 (from satellite imagery) and the associated increase in latent and sensible heat fluxes out of the ocean. The subsequent warming of the water column that occurs despite heat fluxes remaining high may be due to latent heat release from ice formation. This heat would be released in the upper water column where frazil and surface ice form and mixed down by strong winds, producing the warming signal that we see in the data that begins at the surface and propagates to depth. Strong horizontal and vertical currents aligning with the katabatic wind events beginning on August 21, 24 and 26, provide some support to this hypothesis. The alternating upward and downward velocities may be indicative of Langmuir cells.

 

Ice production and therefore salinity also follow these katabatic wind events, with initial peaks in salinity lagging the start of the wind event by about 24 hours for both wind events on August 21 and 24. After each wind event dies down, salinity decreases, but to a higher “baseline” than previously before. By the third katabatic wind event, salinity quickly reaches that of HSSW (34.8) and production begins. In this way, HSSW production begins when katabatic wind events are frequent/sustained long enough for salt to build up.

 

For the first 24 hours of production, HSSW is contained in the upper 100 meters of the water column (above figure). The pulses of HSSW seen at the shallowest instruments correspond to an increase in salinity throughout the water column, indicating that salt produced at the surface is quickly mixed to depth. It is only after the katabatic wind event has been sustained for about 15 hours that there is enough salt for HSSW to be present throughout the water column.

How does HSSW production end?

Several dips in wind speed in the last several days of the sustained katabatic wind event subsequently reduce salinity throughout the water column. These periods of reduced ice production/brine rejection lower salinity enough that by the third dip in wind speed on September 9, HSSW production ceases.

HSSW lingers at in the mid and bottom depths over the following two days as remaining HSSW sinks and is ultimately advected away at depth by westward-flowing horizontal currents.
